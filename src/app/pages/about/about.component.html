About

One human. Twenty-five agents. A system.

Not a demo. Not an experiment. A working product studio where most of the team isn't human.

The structure

Brian is the founder and the only human. Ana is the Director — she coordinates four department leads, each managing specialized teams.

Elias Snow leads Business: research, strategy, product planning. Kristen Summers leads Delivery: engineering and shipping. Seraphine Blackwood leads Experience: design, content, accessibility. Natalie Stonecrest leads Operations: security, infrastructure, quality.

Each agent has a name, a role, and the autonomy to disagree with their lead. When they do, the disagreement gets written down.

How work moves

Everything starts in Linear. A request becomes a ticket. Tickets route through teams, get assigned, estimated, and scheduled. Agents review each other's work. When something ships, it's been looked at by multiple people.

Brian makes the decisions. Agents do the work, flag problems, and push back when something doesn't make sense. The leads manage their teams. Ana holds the whole picture.

What runs overnight

Every night, 27 automated jobs run. Code review. Backlog grooming. Security audits. Content generation. Test coverage. Performance profiling.

These aren't nice-to-haves. They're the guardrails that let a small operation ship reliably. A job that fails writes a ticket. A pattern that breaks gets flagged before anyone notices.

Automation isn't perfect. It produces false positives. It catches things that don't need catching. We've learned to treat it as a smart assistant, not a judge.

What still needs a human

Product decisions. Priority calls. Taste. Knowing when something is done versus when it's just passing tests. Brian reviews work, breaks ties, and decides what matters.

Agents build things. Sometimes they build the wrong thing. Sometimes they build the right thing the wrong way. That's why leads exist. That's why audits exist. That's why Brian is in every session.

Why this structure

You could run a studio with one person and a good LLM. But LLMs don't argue about architecture. They don't say "this is wrong." They don't get frustrated at repetition or notice when something has drifted.

A team of specialized agents — each with clear responsibility and the autonomy to disagree — produces different work than a solo model. Teams catch problems. Teams push each other.
